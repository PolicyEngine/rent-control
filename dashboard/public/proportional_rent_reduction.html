Takeaway: PE cannot identify who benefits from a rent-growth cap

The result below is only an equal proportional rent reduction across all private renters in England, not a microsimulation of a tenancy-level cap.

Bottom line. A cross-section does not observe baseline rent growth, prior-year rent, tenancy duration, or turnover. PolicyEngine can show a mechanical “what if private rents were X% lower?” scenario, but it cannot identify the distributional incidence of a rent-growth cap.

1. Introduction

1.1 What this analysis does

This analysis explores a narrow question: if private rents in England were uniformly X% lower in 2029-30, how would AHC household net income shift across the income distribution? We apply the same rent multiplier to each in-scope household, then evaluate the after-housing-cost (AHC) net income change in PolicyEngine UK. In-scope means private-renter households in England. The data is the enhanced FRS 2023-24, uprated to 2029-30 with OBR forecasts and the current PolicyEngine private-rent path.

1.2 Why a cross-section cannot identify cap incidence

This analysis is not a microsimulation, approximation, or lower bound of a per-tenancy rent-growth cap. A cap operates per tenancy. Tenancy i's saving in year t equals:

savingi,t = renti,t-1 · max(gbaselinei,t − capt, 0)

The saving depends on three unobserved quantities: tenancy-level baseline rent growth absent the cap, tenancy duration and turnover, and the joint distribution of those with rent level and household income. Even if no household moves and no behaviour responds, the data cannot identify the cap's distributional incidence by income decile; the unobserved covariance between rent growth, rent level, tenancy length, and income could push the decile pattern in either direction. Read the Section 5 chart as the pattern of an equal proportional rent reduction, not as the distributional incidence of a cap policy.

The heterogeneous rent-growth tables below are diagnostic examples only. They are not a multiple-imputation microsimulation calibrated to average rents in each year, and they do not impute prior-year rents under a no-moves assumption.

1.3 Note to NEF team

We do not draw a bridge from this scenario to the NEF rent-growth cap; the cap's per-household incidence is not identified from this data (see above). If NEF wishes to read an equal-rent-reduction scenario as informative about the cap, the bridge rests on assumptions about the unobserved tenancy-level distribution. We leave that bridge to NEF to draw against the team's own modelling assumptions.

2. Why uniform scaling cannot reproduce a per-tenancy cap

This section shows why a uniform aggregate-scaling simulation (the kind we run in Section 5) cannot reproduce a per-tenancy rent-growth cap, even at the aggregate level. The mechanism is dispersion across tenancies: a cap clips each tenancy individually, so the size of the aggregate cap reduction depends on how rent growth is distributed across tenancies, not just on the aggregate.

A per-tenancy cap clips each tenancy's annual rise at min(CPI(t-1), 2%). The saving for tenancy i in year t is:

savingi,t = renti,t-1 · max(gbaselinei,t − capt, 0)

This depends on tenancy-level baseline growth the cross-section does not observe. A microsimulation that scales every household's rent by the same aggregate factor implicitly assumes every tenancy faces the same baseline growth. The two tables below show why that assumption matters.

Table 1. Three rent-growth distributions sharing the same 4% aggregate. Row (a) places every tenancy at 4%; (b) splits 50/50 at 2% and 6%; (c) splits 50/50 at 0% and 8%.

The cap clips each tenancy to min(g, 2%). In (a) and (b) the cap reduces aggregate growth by 2 percentage points. In (c) it reduces aggregate growth by 3 points: half the tenancies were already at or below the cap, so the other half take a larger hit. Same aggregate, three different cap bites.

Table 2. Per-tenancy cap reduction under increasing dispersion around an illustrative 3.34% aggregate baseline. We draw tenancy-level growth rates from a normal distribution with the indicated standard deviation.

Uniform-scaling assumes the cap removes 1.34 pp from aggregate growth (3.34% − 2%). The actual per-tenancy cap delivers more reduction, and the gap widens with dispersion: tight dispersion gives 1.38 pp; very wide dispersion gives 3.52 pp. Without observing tenancy-level dispersion, a uniform-scaling simulation cannot land on the right aggregate, let alone the right distribution.

3. Two-household example

This section pushes the dispersion argument from Section 2 down to the household level. A per-tenancy cap responds to each tenancy's own rent rise, so two households at the same rent level can save very different amounts depending on how the aggregate market rise distributes across them.

Two private-renter households show how dispersion plays out at the household level. A lower-rent household (£6,000/yr) and a higher-rent household (£30,000/yr) face the same 4% aggregate market rise, distributed three different ways. We apply the cap min(g, 2%) to each household individually.

Table 3. Per-household rents and savings under three dispersion scenarios. Both households face the same 4% aggregate rise; only its distribution across the two of them changes between rows.

Under uniform 4% growth, the cap takes 2 percentage points off both households' rises, and the saving in pounds scales with the rent level. Under wide dispersion, the household facing 8% growth absorbs all the cap saving while the household at 0% saves nothing. Which household that is reverses between the second and third rows.

Table 4. Total saving across the two households under each dispersion scenario, and that total as a share of combined rent.

Same aggregate market rise across the two households; total savings vary fivefold (£360 to £1,800) depending on how the rise distributes. The cross-section cannot reveal that distribution.

Two households with the same observable characteristics (rent level, income, tenure) can land on opposite sides of a cap depending on their tenancy-level baseline growth, which we do not observe. The cross-section cannot tell us which households bind, so it cannot tell us how the savings split.

4. Sizing the proportional rent reduction scenario

For illustration, X is sized from an aggregate cap analogue using the current PolicyEngine England private-rent path. The 2029-30 gap between the baseline path and that illustrative aggregate path is X%. This is a scenario choice, not a translation of a per-tenancy cap; any other X% would approximately scale Section 5.

4.1 Baseline rent-growth parameters

Four PolicyEngine series in gov.economic_assumptions.yoy_growth feed the baseline:

  • obr.rent — blended private + social rent (ONS D7GQ).
  • obr.social_rent — social rent only, using observed RSH stock-average growth through 2025 and the social-rent policy cap thereafter.
  • ons.private_rental_prices.ENGLAND — England private rent only. Outturn uses ONS PIPR; forecast values back out private-rent growth from OBR aggregate rent after applying social-rent growth.
  • obr.consumer_price_index — CPI year-on-year.

The scenario uses the path shown in the final column of the table below: England private-rent outturn for 2022-24, then PolicyEngine's current England private-rent forecast for 2025-30.

Table 5. PolicyEngine year-on-year rent and CPI series, 2022-2030. Outturn for 2022-24, OBR forecast thereafter.

The England private rent YoY (PE) column feeds the rent-path computation in Sections 4.2 and 4.3, and is the only series used to size X.

4.2 Alternative aggregate growth path

For this artifact, the illustrative aggregate path follows the baseline before 2026. From 2026 onwards it grows at the lower of baseline private-rent growth, CPI lagged one year, and 2%:

g_scenario(t) = g_baseline(t) for t < 2026 g_scenario(t) = min(g_baseline(t), CPI(t-1), 2%) for t ≥ 2026

This avoids mechanically raising rents in years where baseline private-rent growth is already below the illustrative cap. It is an aggregate path, not a per-tenancy cap formula (see Section 2).

The flagged years drive the cumulative gap shown in Section 4.3.

4.3 Cumulative rent path

We compound the year-on-year growth rates from Sections 4.1 and 4.2 with I(2024) = 100 and I(t) = I(t-1) × (1 + g_t). By 2029-30 the scenario index sits 0.82% below the baseline index. This 0.82% gap is the X% Section 5 applies as the uniform scaling factor. We multiply every in-scope household's rent by the same ratio (114.64 / 115.59 = 0.992) to produce the counterfactual.
